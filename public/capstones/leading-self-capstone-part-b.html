Section 03
Outcome 3 · Shame distinguished from guilt

Shame, Distinguished from Guilt

Why is what you named in Section 01 shame -- not guilt? And why does the distinction matter for how you lead?
Guilt: I did something wrong. Shame: something is wrong with me. Guilt can be repaired through action; shame requires renaming what you think is true about yourself. The distinction is the foundation of working with this honestly.

Digital Edge · AI Application

Your 90-day Resilience plan from six weeks of work

Week 6 standardised Digital Edge for this Capstone
Why this matters more than the Capstone itself: The Capstone is an input. A plan is an output. Practitioners finish programmes with notes, not plans. This is the conversion moment -- the prompt that takes everything (your Mindset Action Plan, your Trigger Map, your Accountability Brief, your peer reflections) and produces one page. Three 30-day blocks. The document you reference for the next 90 days.
The prompt (copy-paste into Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini)
You are a leadership coach. I'll paste four things below: my Mindset Action Plan (Capstone Part A), my Trigger Map (Practical 5), my Accountability Brief (Practical 6), and reflections from my peer sessions. Synthesise them into a one-page 90-day Resilience Plan organised as three 30-day blocks. For each 30-day block, name: (1) the one practice I'm building this block, (2) the one trigger response I'm reinforcing, (3) the one accountability move I'm making with my partner, (4) one observable measure that tells me whether this block is working. Use my language, not template language. Keep it shorter than I'll want it to be. Material: [paste Mindset Action Plan, Trigger Map, Accountability Brief, peer reflections]
How to apply this to your Capstone: Run the prompt with your Capstone Part A, your Practical 5, your Practical 6, and any peer session notes as input. Compare the AI's 90-day plan with what you wrote in Section 08 above. The four reflection questions below ask you to evidence your evaluative judgement -- this is Outcome 6 evidence, and the difference between distinction and pass is what you applied versus what you critically rejected.

Take This to Peer-to-Peer

The last peer conversation of the Journey.

The Resilience Action Plan is the most synthesised piece of work you've produced. Polish can hide what's still loose. A peer who's worked through their own version can hear which parts are genuinely held together and which are stapled. The conversation is the test.

What to bring

Read Sections 01, 04, 05, and 07 aloud, in order. Then read the three legs in Section 06. Don't summarise -- read the actual content. The test is whether each section sounds like it belongs to the same person -- whether the carrying, the body, the trigger map, the partner, and the becoming sentence all hold together as one operating system.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • The shame pattern you named -- is it the real one, or the one you were able to write about because you've already half-resolved it? What's the harder version you didn't quite name?
  • Of the three legs of the stool, which one is most likely to break first under pressure -- and what's the early warning sign you'll watch for?
  • The becoming sentence -- read it aloud once more. Did it sound like you, or did it sound like the version of you that finishes a leadership programme? If it's the second, what would the first version say instead?
