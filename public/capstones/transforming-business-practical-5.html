The Drift Heatmap

Score the transformation operating system across five rhythms -- decision cadence, communication cadence, escalation discipline, learning cadence, reset trigger -- as green/amber/red against observable evidence in the past 60 days. Pick the genuinely most-drifted rhythm, not the safest one to talk about. Then design the reset move that addresses the cause, not the symptom -- and book it in the calendar with named participants and a 30-day success measure. The reset is the work; the heatmap is the diagnostic.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • Each rhythm scored against observable evidence from the past 60 days, not against gut feel or against the dashboard you wish you were running.
  • The most-drifted rhythm is the genuinely most-drifted one -- including if naming it surfaces a leadership cadence you have been letting slip personally.
  • The reset move addresses cause, not symptom. If the move is 'more meetings', 'longer agendas', or 'harder targets', it is escalation, not reset.
  • The reset is booked in the calendar with named participants, a specific date, and an observable success measure for 30 days from now.
  • The 30-day success measure is something a peer could check on the date -- not a feeling, not 'I will know it when I see it'.
1
Step 1
Frame the System You Are Scoring

Before you score, name the system. Not a generic transformation -- the specific operating system of the AI-era business transformation you are leading right now. Specificity here protects against scoring the version of the system you wish you were running.

01
The Operating System
The transformation programme. Two sentences. What it is, who runs it day-to-day, what the formal rhythms are.
02
The 60-Day Window
What real events from the past 60 days will you score against? Two or three brief markers.
2
Step 2
Score the Five Rhythms

Score each rhythm green, amber, or red against what you have actually observed in the past 60 days. For each one, write the specific evidence -- the meeting that did or did not happen, the decision that did or did not get made, the signal that was or was not picked up. Score against evidence, not against the version of the system you want to defend in this audit.

Green: holding
Amber: drift visible
Red: drifted, costing
01
Decision Cadence
Decisions land in the rhythm they are scheduled to land in. Items do not slip from steering committee to steering committee. Decisions made are decisions executed -- not decisions to revisit next time.
Score
02
Communication Cadence
The vision and the operational state are being communicated to the layers that matter, in the rhythm they are meant to be. Town halls happen, briefings land, the field hears from the centre on the schedule that was promised -- and the message that arrives is the one that was sent.
Score
03
Escalation Discipline
Issues escalate to the right level at the right time -- not too early (overwhelming the executive), not too late (after the regulator has noticed, after the petition has been filed, after the board has read about it). Escalation goes through the rhythm, not around it.
Score
04
Learning Cadence
The transformation has a rhythm for learning from what is happening -- not just monitoring what is happening. Patterns are being read, signals from the field are travelling back to the centre, the post-mortem on each phase is producing the next phase, not just the current report.
Score
05
Reset Trigger
The system has a rhythm for noticing when it is drifting and triggering a reset -- not waiting for a crisis to force it. A reset trigger is the rhythm that says 'the original plan is no longer the right plan' before the regulator, the board, or the petition does it for you.
Score
3
Step 3
Pick the Most-Drifted Rhythm and Address the Cause

If two or more rhythms scored red, pick the one whose drift is producing the most cost in the rest of the system. The most-drifted rhythm is rarely the one that is easiest to talk about. Before you design the reset move, name the cause -- the underlying mechanism producing the drift, not the symptom you can see. The reset has to address the cause. If it addresses the symptom, you are escalating, not resetting.

The Most-Drifted Rhythm
Which one, and what is the cause?
Name the rhythm. Then name the cause -- the underlying mechanism producing the drift. The cause is not 'we are too busy' or 'people are not engaged'. It is the structural reason the rhythm has slipped.

AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Reset

Drift Heatmaps fail in two predictable places -- the practitioner picks the safest rhythm to talk about (rather than the genuinely most-drifted), and the reset move escalates the original ambition rather than addressing the cause. AI as an outside reader catches both. Use it before you submit.

1
Test Whether You Picked the Safest Rhythm
Ask AI to test whether the most-drifted rhythm you named is the genuinely most-drifted one -- or the safest one to surface to leadership.
Paste this prompt
I have scored five operating rhythms (decision cadence, communication cadence, escalation discipline, learning cadence, reset trigger) green/amber/red. My scores and evidence are: [paste]. The most-drifted rhythm I picked is: [paste]. Pressure-test on three criteria. (1) Of the rhythms I scored red or amber, which one would surface a leadership cadence I have personally been letting slip -- and is that the one I picked, or did I pick a different red? (2) Of the rhythms I scored green, is there one where the evidence I cited is process-evidence (the meeting happened) rather than outcome-evidence (the meeting produced what it was meant to)? (3) If a Group CFO read this heatmap, which rhythm would they ask to drill into first -- and is that the one I have foregrounded, or have I steered them toward the safer one? Tell me where the read is defensive.
Where did the read go defensive? What is the rhythm you should have foregrounded?
2
Test Whether the Reset Move Addresses Cause
Ask AI to test whether the reset move addresses the cause -- or whether it is escalation dressed as reset.
Paste this prompt
My most-drifted rhythm is [paste]. The cause I named is [paste]. The reset move I designed is [paste]. The 30-day success measure is [paste]. Pressure-test on three criteria. (1) Could the reset move be described as 'more of what we already do' (more meetings, longer agendas, harder targets) -- or does it introduce a new mechanism the system has not been running? (2) Does the success measure check whether the cause has been addressed, or only whether the reset move has happened (process versus outcome again)? (3) If the reset move runs as planned for 30 days but the rhythm does not change, what was the design flaw? Tell me where the move is escalation, not reset, and rewrite the success measure with cause-level verification.
Where is the move escalation? What is the rewritten success measure?
AI Lens
Six months from now, when the Q3 Plan Reality Check has run twice and the system has had two opportunities to trigger a reset without a crisis -- which rhythm will have started drifting in the cover that the new mechanism creates? And what is the second-order reset trigger that should override the first if that happens?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Drift Heatmaps fall apart at the same place every time -- the practitioner picks the safest rhythm to surface (often Reset Trigger because it puts the gap on the system not on the leader), and the reset move is escalation in the language of reset. A peer who has scored their own system can hear both at the same speed an assessor will. Bring this to your peer-to-peer call this week.

What to bring

Read out the five rhythm scores first, with the evidence for each. If your peer's first reaction is 'all the reds are about the system, none are about you' -- the heatmap is defensive and at least one score needs revisiting. Then read the reset move and the success measure. Ask your peer whether the move would change the rhythm in 30 days, or whether the rhythm could absorb the move without changing.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • Of the rhythms you scored green -- is the evidence process-evidence (the meeting happened) or outcome-evidence (the meeting produced what it was meant to)? If the green is process-only, it is at risk of being amber.
  • The most-drifted rhythm you picked -- is it the one that puts the gap most uncomfortably on a leader (you, your sponsor, your peer) rather than on the system? If it is on the system, you may have picked the safer one.
  • Your reset move -- if the steering committee runs it as designed and the rhythm does not change in 30 days, what was the design flaw? If you cannot answer that in advance, the success measure is process-only.
