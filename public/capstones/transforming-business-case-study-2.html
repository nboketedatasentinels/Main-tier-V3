The case · read first
Safaricom Kenya, 2024-2025 -- and the November 2025 petition
Collapse
Background

Safaricom is Kenya's largest mobile network operator, headquartered in Nairobi, and the parent company of M-Pesa -- the mobile money service launched in March 2007 that transformed financial inclusion in Kenya from 26% in 2006 to 84.8% by 2024. Group CEO Dr. Peter Ndegwa has led Safaricom through a $500 million Vision 2030 transformation programme. Esther Waititu serves as Chief Financial Services Officer and James Maitai as Chief Technology Information Officer. M-Pesa accounts for 44.2% of Safaricom's total service revenue in Kenya, with over 35 million active users, more than 100 million transactions daily, and a network of 300,000 agents and 1.9 million merchants.

Across 2024 and 2025, Safaricom rolled out an integrated AI strategy across customer service, fraud detection, network operations, and product development. The flagship customer-facing AI deployment is Zuri, a generative AI chatbot built on Amazon Bedrock and SageMaker, serving 30+ million M-Pesa users. Internal documentation positioned Zuri as augmentation -- handling routine queries, freeing human agents for complex issues. The Fintech 2.0 platform migration occurred on 22 September 2025, with all 35 million users moved to a cloud-native, AI-native architecture in a single overnight window.

In November 2025, Victor Odhiambo filed a petition in the Nairobi High Court against Safaricom and four respondents (the Attorney-General, the Communications Authority of Kenya, the Competition Authority of Kenya, and the Kenya Consumer Protection Advisory Committee). The petition alleges that Safaricom's AI ecosystem -- specifically Zuri and the automated decision-making systems behind M-Pesa transaction approvals, reversals, and account restrictions -- violates constitutional rights including privacy, fair administrative action, and consumer protection.

The petition's central allegation is operationally specific. It states that customers calling 100 and 234 customer service lines are routed to Zuri without guaranteed access to a human agent. Zuri 'acts as a gatekeeper, limiting escalation to human intervention even in urgent cases involving fraud, mistaken transfers, or compromised accounts.' Court documents highlight that Safaricom processes over 100 million M-Pesa transactions daily, with automated systems handling approvals, reversals, and account restrictions in real time. The petition references Kenya's National AI Strategy 2025-2030, which acknowledges gaps in the legal framework, while arguing that large-scale AI deployment in essential services has continued without sufficient safeguards.

Inside Safaricom, public statements from CTIO Maitai and CFSO Waititu through 2025 framed the AI integration as 'enhancing operational efficiency' and 'enabling faster, more agile product launches.' The internal vision sentence in 2024-2025 publications was repeatable: 'AI-native infrastructure for Africa's digital economy.' Yet the resistance that emerged -- from a single Kenyan citizen filing a constitutional petition -- was not reflected in any internal customer feedback channels or stakeholder engagement reporting prior to the filing.

The dynamic to analyse

This case is not a story about whether Safaricom's AI deployment was technically capable. By external measures, the M-Pesa platform performance metrics improved substantially -- transaction capacity rose from 4,500 to 6,000 transactions per second with peak capacity to 12,000, fraud detection accuracy reached 89% (F1 score), and the September 2025 Fintech 2.0 migration succeeded. It is a story about a senior practitioner team that read the resistance signals through a single category -- adoption-aversion -- when the resistance was actually informed risk and rights-based concern that escalated into legal action.

The questions below ask you to analyse where the vision sentence drifted between executive statements and operational reality, identify which resistance categories were misread, and design the operating system reset that should have been triggered before the petition was filed.

Question 01
Outcome 3 · Vision portability

Did the internal vision sentence survive 24-hour recall among M-Pesa users?

Safaricom's internal vision sentence in 2024-2025 was 'AI-native infrastructure for Africa's digital economy.' Did this sentence survive the 24-hour recall test among M-Pesa users and customer service-facing employees -- and what version of the vision were users carrying that ultimately surfaced in the High Court petition?
The 24-hour recall test asks what version of the vision is being carried, not what was articulated. The user-recall version is read from case evidence -- the petition language, the customer service routing pattern, what users were complaining about. The gap between the two versions is the answer; collapsing the internal vision and the user-recall version into the same thing is the failure mode.

Question 02
Outcome 4 · Resistance category

Distinguish the four resistance categories -- and identify the misread

The resistance that culminated in the November 2025 petition had been visible in customer service interaction patterns for at least 12 months. Which of the four resistance categories -- informed risk, fear, identity, political -- did Safaricom's leadership misread, and which category was the resistance actually in?
The four categories are course content -- the question asks you to apply the discipline. The misread and the actual category are different categories, named separately. The distinction has to be structural -- what each category produces in the resistance behaviour, not just emotional flavour. Case evidence anchors the call: the named respondents in the petition, the constitutional rights basis, the regulatory framing.

Outcome 5 · Drift heatmap

Score the five rhythms -- and name the reset move

Score Safaricom's AI rollout operating system on the five rhythms -- decision cadence, communication cadence, escalation discipline, learning cadence, reset trigger -- as it was operating in mid-2025. Which rhythm had drifted most, and what specific reset move should have been triggered before the petition filing?
All five rhythms scored against case evidence -- not assigned colours generically. The most-drifted rhythm is identified explicitly. The reset move addresses cause, not symptom: a process change with named participants and a date, not 'better customer service'. The test is whether the reset move could have been triggered before the petition filing on the evidence available at the time.
