The Transformation Operating Model

A six-section operating model for the AI-era transformation. The AI Operating Hour discipline (Week 1). The Transformation Thesis (Week 2). The Capability Map (Week 3). The Data Foundation Audit (Week 4 — the heaviest week). The Adoption Curve Read (Week 5). The AI Integration section (judgement, not adoption). One running document, 1,500–2,000 words. Plus the Lessons Synthesis paragraph that names what has integrated across the six weeks.

Capstone · Week 6 · Single Artefact · Closes Module 2
Innovation and AI for Digital Transformation is the only LIFT pillar with a single-Capstone structure. The six-section Operating Model is one running document, not two paired plans. 50% of the competence pass weighting sits on this artefact. Submission triggers the 7,000-point Module 2 payout.
What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • The six sections read as one running document with through-lines between them — not six exercises stapled together.
  • Each section draws on the actual Practical from its corresponding week — same use case, refined and integrated.
  • Total length is 1,500–2,000 words.
  • The Operating Model could be handed to an incoming CEO with no context, and would survive scrutiny without the practitioner in the room.
  • The AI Integration section names what was kept, what was rejected, and why — evaluative judgement, not summary.
  • The Lessons Synthesis names a pattern the practitioner did not see clearly in Week 1.

AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Capstone Before You Submit

Operating Models fail in two predictable places — the six sections sit beside each other rather than building on each other, and the Lessons Synthesis is reflection-language rather than pattern-language. The Capstone is 50% of the competence pass; use AI before submission.

Test Whether the Six Sections Read as One Document
Paste this prompt
Six-section Operating Model: (1) AI Operating Hour, (2) Thesis, (3) Capability Map, (4) Foundation Audit, (5) Adoption Curve Read, (6) AI Integration. Full Model: [paste]. Pressure-test: (1) Do the sections build on each other or sit beside each other? (2) If the executive read only sections 4 and 5, would they understand the connection to sections 2 and 3? (3) Are there sentences that could have been written in Week 1?
Test the Lessons Synthesis for Pattern, Not Reflection
Paste this prompt
My Synthesis paragraph: [paste]. Pressure-test: (1) Could this have been written in Week 1 with 'I am about to learn' replacing 'I learned'? (2) Does it reference at least four of the six practicals by their actual content? (3) Is the pattern specific to how the six pieces of work integrated, or a paraphrase of the Journey description? Tell me where the Synthesis is reflection, not pattern.
AI Lens
Twelve months from now, when the foundation work has run for nine of the twelve months it needs and the conduct-complaint multiple has either passed or failed the runnable test — which section of this Operating Model will have aged worst, and which will have held?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Operating Models fall apart at the same place every time — the six sections sit beside each other rather than building on each other, and the Lessons Synthesis is reflection-language rather than pattern-language. This is the single Capstone of the Journey — 50% of the competence pass weighting — bring it with extra time scheduled.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • The six sections — if you covered up the section labels, could your peer still tell which section was which from the language alone?
  • The AI Integration section — is the judgement evaluative (kept, rejected, why) or descriptive (summarises what AI suggested)?
  • The Lessons Synthesis — can your peer state the pattern back to you in one sentence in their own words?
