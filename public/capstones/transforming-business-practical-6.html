Capstone Part B · Week 6 · Closes the Combined Capstone
This Memo is also Part B of your Combined Capstone -- the operating document that holds the work together after the Connection Blueprint has built the buy-in. Both parts are weighted equally within the 50% Capstone allocation. Write this once. Write it for the named executive. Write it as if you are about to send it.
What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • The Memo is sendable -- the named executive could read it and act on it without asking you for context.
  • The vision section is the version that survived the 24-hour recall test, not the version that lands well in slides.
  • The resistance map distinguishes the four categories (informed risk / fear / identity / political) and names a category-matched move for each significant resister -- no 'manage resistance' generic line.
  • The reset moves address cause not symptom. Cause-fix can be checked by a peer; symptom-fix dresses up escalation as reset.
  • The executive ask has a specific decision, a specific date, and a specific consequence of inaction.
  • The Lessons Synthesis names a pattern the practitioner did not see in Week 1 -- not professional-development reflection.

Step 2
Write the Five Sections

Write the Memo as if you are about to send it to the named sponsor this afternoon. Each section is short -- the whole Memo is 800 to 1,000 words. The five sections are sequential. Vision before Resistance. Resistance before Reset. Reset before Cadence. Cadence before Ask. The order is the operating logic of the transformation -- it is also the order the sponsor's eye will travel down the page.

AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Memo Before You Send

Transformation Memos fail in two predictable places -- the Memo reads as a status update with sections (vision-update, resistance-update, reset-update) rather than as one operating document, and the executive ask is dressed-up awareness rather than a real decision request. AI as an outside reader catches both. Use it before you submit.

1
Test Whether the Five Sections Read as One Memo
Ask AI to test whether the sections form a connected operating document or six exercises stapled together.
Paste this prompt
I am submitting a five-section Transformation Memo for an executive sponsor: vision, resistance map, system reset moves, operating cadence, executive ask. The Memo is: [paste]. Pressure-test on three criteria. (1) Reading the Memo top to bottom, do the five sections build on each other -- does the resistance map reference the vision, does the reset reference the resistance, does the cadence reference the reset, does the ask reference the cadence -- or do the sections sit beside each other without connection? (2) If a sponsor read only the executive ask without the rest of the Memo, would they understand what they are being asked to decide and why? (3) Are there any sentences in the Memo that could have been written in Week 1 -- before the Practicals data existed -- which would suggest the sections have not actually drawn on the weekly work? Tell me where the Memo is six exercises rather than one document, and where it could be tightened.
Where does the Memo not yet read as one document?
2
Test the Lessons Synthesis for Pattern, Not Reflection
Ask AI to test whether the Synthesis names a real cross-practical pattern or a generic professional development paragraph.
Paste this prompt
My Lessons Synthesis paragraph reads: [paste]. Pressure-test on three criteria. (1) Could this paragraph have been written in Week 1 of the Journey, before any of the practicals were complete -- with the words 'I am about to learn' replacing 'I learned'? If yes, it is not a Synthesis, it is a learning intention. (2) Does the paragraph reference at least three of the six practicals by their actual content (the Door Practice room reading, the Connection Blueprint audience match, the Vision Sentence recall, the Resistance Reframe categorisation, the Drift Heatmap rhythm scoring, the Memo AI integration), or does it stay at 'I learned to listen, plan, and reflect'? (3) Is the pattern the practitioner names something a reader would not have predicted from the Journey title alone -- something specific about how the six pieces of work integrated -- or is it a paraphrase of the Journey description? Tell me where the Synthesis is reflection, not pattern.
Where is the Synthesis reflection rather than pattern? What is the rewritten version?
AI Lens
Six months from now, when the Memo's reset moves have run for two quarters and the Reality Check has been at risk of theatre at least once, which section of this Memo will have aged worst -- and which will have held? And what does that prediction say about which discipline you have actually integrated versus which is still scaffolding?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Transformation Memos fall apart at the same place every time -- the five sections sit beside each other rather than building on each other, and the Synthesis is reflection-language rather than pattern-language. A peer who has written their own Memo can hear both at the same speed an assessor will. This is the Capstone Part B submission -- it triggers the second 7,000-point payout. Bring it to your peer-to-peer call this week.

What to bring

Read out the executive ask first -- before any of the other sections. Ask your peer whether they understand what is being asked, by when, and what changes if it does not happen. If they cannot answer all three from the ask alone, the ask is not yet sendable. Then read the Lessons Synthesis. If your peer's first reaction is 'this could have been written in Week 1' -- it is reflection, not pattern. If they say 'this names something specific that I have to push back on or agree with' -- it is pattern, and the Synthesis is doing its work.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • The five sections -- if you covered up the section labels, could your peer still tell which section was which from the language alone? If sections 3 and 4 sound interchangeable, the operating logic has not yet landed.
  • The executive ask -- if your peer pretended to be the named sponsor, would they reply 'I understand the decision' or would they reply 'I need more context first'? If the second, the ask is not yet sendable.
  • The Lessons Synthesis -- can your peer state the pattern back to you in one sentence using their own words? If they can, the pattern is integrated. If they have to quote you, the pattern is still language, not yet a discipline.
