The Connection Blueprint

A four-section persuasion artefact -- Position, Now, Stake, Survival -- written for one named executive whose decision is the one that matters in the next 90 days. Not a generic transformation message. A piece of writing that an executive could read in seven minutes and act on. This Practical and the Capstone Part A are the same artefact -- the Blueprint you submit here triggers the 7,000-point Module 1 payout.

Capstone Part A · Week 2
This Blueprint is also Part A of your Combined Capstone. One artefact, two uses -- weekly Practical and summative submission. Write it once, write it for the named executive, write it as if you are about to send it.
What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • The audience is a specific real executive -- by role, ideally name -- not 'the leadership team' or 'the board'.
  • The Now anchors to a specific business cycle moment: a board cycle, a regulatory window, a competitor move. Not generic 'urgency'.
  • The Stake is named in the audience's language -- the consequence they care about, not the consequence the practitioner wants them to care about.
  • The Survival section names what survival of the transformation requires from this audience in the next 90 days. Specific, time-bound, accountable. Not vision language.
  • A peer reading the Blueprint can predict what the named executive would say in response. If they cannot, the audience match is not yet there.
1
Step 1
Name the Audience and the Moment

Before the four sections, name the audience -- specifically. Name what is true about their week that an outsider would not know. The Blueprint is only as good as the audience it is written for. A generic Blueprint is the same as no Blueprint.

The Audience
Who, by role and name, would read this Blueprint?
Pick one real executive whose decision is the one that matters for the transformation in the next 90 days. The CEO, the COO, the Group CFO, the Chair of the Audit Committee, the Group Risk Director. Not the leadership team. One person.
The Moment
What about right now makes this the right moment for this Blueprint to land?
Not 'now is urgent' -- specifics. The board cycle, the regulator window, the competitor move, the executive transition, the budget cycle. The moment a peer reading this could verify externally.
2
Step 2
Write the Four Sections

Write the Blueprint as if you are about to send it to the named executive this afternoon. Each section is short -- two to three short paragraphs at most. The four are sequential. Position before Now. Now before Stake. Stake before Survival. The order is the persuasion logic. Resist the temptation to lead with Survival ('what I need from you') -- that is the section the audience will read last.

The Connection Blueprint
Four sections · in order · written for one named executive · sendable as is
01
Position
Where the transformation programme stands today, with specifics. Not the executive summary version -- the version that names the actual state. What is true. What is working. What is not. In the audience's frame of reference, not yours.
02
Now
Why this moment, specifically. The business cycle moment that makes this the right time to act, not generic urgency. A peer reading should be able to verify the moment externally -- a published board calendar, a regulator deadline, a competitor announcement.
03
Stake
What is at stake for the named executive specifically. Not the organisation in the abstract -- the audience's accountability, in the audience's language. What survives or fails for them, depending on what they decide.
04
Survival
What survival of the transformation requires from this audience in the next 90 days. Specific. Time-bound. Accountable. The decision, the moment, the consequence of inaction. This is the section that has to be sendable -- the executive should be able to read it and know what is being asked of them, by when, and what changes for them if they do or do not act.
3
Step 3
Test the Audience Match Before You Submit

The Blueprint is only as good as how well it lands with its named audience. Two checks. First: read each section back asking 'is this in the executive's language, or my language?' Second: predict what the executive would say in response to the Survival ask. If you cannot predict the response, the Blueprint is not yet matched -- you are writing into uncertainty about how the audience will receive it. Both checks are honest, not heroic.

01
Language Match
Which of the four sections is most in your language and least in the executive's? Honest. Most Blueprints are weakest in Position because the practitioner defaults to programme-narrative.
02
Predicted Response
If you sent this Blueprint this afternoon, what would the named executive say in their reply? Specific.
AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Blueprint

Connection Blueprints fail in two predictable places -- the Now section reads as generic urgency rather than verifiable business cycle moment, and the Survival ask is vague enough that the executive can read it as 'awareness' rather than 'decision'. AI as an outside reader catches both. Use it before you submit.

1
Test the Now Against External Verifiability
Ask AI to test whether the Now section anchors to something a peer or assessor could verify externally -- not to a feeling of urgency.
Paste this prompt
I am writing a four-section Connection Blueprint for a senior executive. The Now section reads: [paste]. Pressure-test it on three criteria. (1) Does the Now name a specific date or window that a peer could verify externally -- a board calendar, a regulator deadline, a competitor announcement, a financial reporting cycle? (2) Is the urgency in the Now derived from the named moment, or is it a separate claim of urgency the moment is meant to support? (3) If you stripped out every adjective and every word that could be replaced with 'soon' or 'critical', is the Now still a Now? Tell me where the section is in feeling-language and rewrite the weakest sentence with a verifiable anchor.
Where did the Now go abstract? What is the rewritten version?
2
Test the Survival Section as a Decision Document
Ask AI to test whether the Survival section is sendable -- whether the executive could read it and know what they are being asked to decide, by when, and what changes if they do or do not act.
Paste this prompt
My Survival section reads: [paste]. Pretend you are the named executive ([role]) reading this for the first time. Test on three criteria. (1) After reading the section, can you list the specific decisions being asked of you, with dates? (2) Is the consequence of inaction in the section -- not implied, stated -- so you understand what changes for you if you do nothing? (3) Are the asks calibrated -- does the practitioner know the difference between a decision-level ask, an awareness-level ask, and a delegation-level ask? Tell me where the Survival section is asking for awareness when it should be asking for a decision, or asking for a decision when it should be delegating.
Where is the calibration off? What is the rewritten Survival section?
AI Lens
If the named executive reads this Blueprint and decides not to act on Survival -- not because they disagree but because they did not feel the Stake landed -- which section is the one that failed to do its work? And what is the version of that section that would have made the decision unavoidable?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Connection Blueprints fall apart at the same place every time -- the practitioner can write four credible sections, but a peer reading them cannot predict what the named executive would say in reply. If your peer cannot predict the response, the Blueprint is not yet matched. Bring this to your peer-to-peer call this week.

What to bring

Read out the Audience naming first -- the role, the name, the priority this quarter. Then read the Survival section. Ask your peer to predict the response. If they say 'I would need more context to predict' -- the Blueprint is not yet sendable, because a real audience would not have more context than your peer at this point. If they say 'they would say yes to (1) and bounce (2) to Group Strategy' -- the Blueprint is matched and your test of the predicted response should match theirs.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • The named executive -- if your peer Googled the role and the moment, would the moment verify? If not, the Now is in feeling-language not in fact.
  • The Survival section -- can your peer state the three asks back to you with dates and consequences, or do they have to re-read it twice? If they re-read it, the executive will too -- and the executive will not have a second read.
  • The predicted response you wrote -- does it match what your peer would predict? If they diverge, one of you is reading the audience wrong, and that is the conversation worth having before submission.
