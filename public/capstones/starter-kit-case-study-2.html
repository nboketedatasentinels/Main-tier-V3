The case · read first
SARS Modernisation, 2007-2018 — and the four-year silence
Collapse
Background

In 2007, the South African Revenue Service (SARS) launched a programme called Modernisation — a multi-year, in-house digitisation effort to move the country's tax collection system from a largely paper-based operation to an automated digital platform. The programme was led by Barry Hore, then SARS Chief Operating Officer. By 2014, Modernisation had transformed SARS into one of the most respected tax authorities on the African continent. Compliance rates had risen, e-filing had become standard, and the programme was on track to complete by 2015.

On 27 September 2014, Tom Moyane was appointed Commissioner of SARS. On 12 December 2014 — three months into his tenure — a newsflash appeared on the internal SARS communication system announcing that the Modernisation programme had been scrapped. There was no consultation with the senior IT leadership team. Hore had not been informed. SARS official Linda Burger, testifying later, described the moment in these terms: more than 66 million rand of in-flight projects were placed at immediate risk; over 22 million rand in assets captured in the e-Central system were subsequently never accounted for; and, in her words:

"It was just like the curtain had fallen."

In July 2015, Moyane signed a contract worth more than 200 million rand with the global advisory firm Gartner to review and replace the IT strategy. The contract was awarded through a closed, non-competitive tender that did not meet National Treasury procurement regulations. A separate Bain and Company contract for business model restructuring cost a further 164 million rand. Hore resigned from SARS shortly after Moyane's arrival. He was not consulted on the halt of his own programme.

In 2018, after Moyane's suspension, retired Judge Robert Nugent was appointed by President Cyril Ramaphosa to head a Commission of Inquiry into Tax Administration and Governance at SARS. Hore returned to give public testimony. So did Andre Rabie, Andre Scheepers, Linda Burger, and other senior IT executives who had remained at SARS through the dismantling. The Nugent final report, delivered in December 2018, found that the cost to SARS to fix what had been dismantled was approximately 1 billion rand. In 2023, the US SEC separately announced a settlement with Gartner under the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act in connection with the SARS contract.

The dynamic to analyse

This case is not about state capture. It is about senior practitioners who watched a working transformation programme being dismantled in real time, were not consulted, and did not surface their objections to the right authority at the right time. They surfaced them four years later, to a judicial commission, when the cost was already 1 billion rand.
