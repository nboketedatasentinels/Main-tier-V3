Stakeholder Briefing Script

Draft the script for a real conversation you'd have this week with the decision-maker from Practical 2 -- briefing them on a difficult risk from your register. Lead with the risk before they ask. Make the ask explicit. Prepare for the pushback. Ready to test in a real conversation, not just to read on a page.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • Risk surfaced in the first 30 seconds. Not buried after good news, not framed as an opportunity.
  • The ask is explicit. Specifically what you need from this stakeholder -- a decision, a sign-off, a connection, time on the agenda.
  • Pushback prepared for. You've thought through what they'll say and you have a response that doesn't fold.
  • Defensible to the actual decision-maker from Practical 2 -- not a generic stakeholder.
1
Step 1
Set the Conversation

Same decision-maker as Practical 2. Pick one risk from your Practical 4 register that you actually need to brief them on -- the one that's most likely to surface in the next two weeks if you don't address it now.

01
Decision-Maker
From Practical 2. The role and the relationship -- in one sentence.
02
The Risk You're Briefing On
From Practical 4. Re-state in one sentence -- specific, not general.
2
Step 2
Write the Lede

The first 30 seconds determine the conversation. Most practitioners open with reassurance -- "things are going well, but..." -- which signals you're hiding something. Open with the risk in plain language. The stakeholder will trust you more, not less.

SAY (30 seconds)
The opening line of the conversation -- in your own voice, as you'd actually say it.
Start with the risk. Not "I want to give you an update on the project," but "I need to flag a risk that needs your decision this week." Then one sentence on what the risk is and why you're raising it now.
3
Step 3
The Substance

Three things the decision-maker needs to hear after the lede. Severity and likelihood (you've thought about it). What you've already done (you're not asking them to fix it for you). And the specific ask (you know what you need from them).

SAY (60 seconds)
Severity and likelihood -- in one sentence each.
Don't say "high risk." Say what specifically would happen if it materialised -- in business terms, not project terms.
SAY (60 seconds)
What you've already done -- two specific things.
This is what separates you from someone delivering bad news. You're not handing them a problem -- you're updating them on action you've already taken.
SAY (30 seconds)
The specific ask -- one sentence.
What you need from this stakeholder. A decision, a meeting, a phone call to a peer, sign-off on a path. Be specific.
4
Step 4
Prepare for Pushback

Before the meeting, write down the two most likely pushback responses -- and your reply. Senior practitioners who haven't done this fold the first time the stakeholder challenges the ask. The script is what holds you steady when the room shifts.

PUSHBACK 1
What they're most likely to push back with.
In their voice. Be specific to the actual person. "Why don't the three of them just sort it out themselves?" or "Can't this wait until next month?"
YOUR RESPONSE
What you'd actually say -- without folding.
Don't apologise. Don't soften. Acknowledge the point and re-anchor the ask.
PUSHBACK 2
The second-most-likely pushback. Often more subtle -- a question that sounds reasonable but would slow the decision.
YOUR RESPONSE
What you'd actually say.
5
Step 5
Close the Conversation

Most stakeholder briefings end without a clear next step. The practitioner walks out unsure what was agreed. The closing line of your script is what locks the outcome.

SAY (15 seconds)
The closing line -- locking the next step.
Confirm the decision, the timing, and what you'll do next. One sentence each.
AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Rehearse the Conversation

Briefing scripts that read well on the page often fall apart when the stakeholder pushes back in unexpected ways. AI can simulate the conversation -- including the angles you haven't prepared for.

1
Roleplay the Stakeholder
Ask AI to play the decision-maker and respond to your script. Push for them to behave like a real senior person -- distracted, time-constrained, sceptical.
Paste this prompt
I'm rehearsing a stakeholder briefing. The decision-maker is a [paste decision-maker summary from Step 1]. The risk I'm briefing on is: [paste Step 1 risk]. My script is: [paste your Step 2 lede + Step 3 substance + Step 5 closing]. Play the decision-maker. Respond as they would -- distracted, time-pressured, asking the inconvenient question. Don't be polite. Be the senior person who has 5 minutes for this and three other things on her mind. Push back on the weakest part of my script.
Where did the script weaken under pressure? What will you tighten?
2
Find the Question You're Hoping They Won't Ask
Ask AI to surface the question your script doesn't answer -- the one you're secretly hoping doesn't come up.
Paste this prompt
Here's a stakeholder briefing script: [paste your Steps 2-5]. Read it as someone looking for what's been left out. What's the question this stakeholder might ask that the script doesn't answer? Specifically -- where does the script gloss over a weakness, sidestep an inconvenient detail, or leave a gap a sceptical person would notice? Give me the single hardest question I haven't prepared for.
What's the question? How will you handle it if it comes up?

Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Briefing scripts are where senior practitioners most often write the conversation they wish they were having, not the one they're actually about to have. Reading the script aloud to a peer surfaces the difference fast.

What to bring

Read your Step 2 lede, Step 3 substance, and Step 5 closing aloud as if you were actually in the meeting. Don't paraphrase. Read it word-for-word. Your peer plays the stakeholder -- their job is to push back on the spots where the script feels rehearsed rather than honest.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • Read your lede aloud once more. Did it sound like a leader naming a risk -- or like someone hoping the risk doesn't really matter?
  • Of your two prepared pushbacks, which one are you most worried they'll actually use? And does your response to it hold up when said aloud?
  • If the stakeholder said yes immediately -- no pushback -- would you actually trust the yes? Or does your script secretly assume you have to convince them?
