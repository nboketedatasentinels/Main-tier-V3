Background

On 4 February 2014, Satya Nadella became the third CEO of Microsoft, succeeding Steve Ballmer. He inherited a company that had become, by his own account, internally combative. Microsoft engineers and product leaders had spent more than a decade in a culture where being seen as the smartest person in the room was the path to promotion. Meetings were performances. Disagreement was sport.

In his 2017 book Hit Refresh, Nadella describes the leadership pattern he found in himself when he stepped into the CEO role. The pattern was not the company's pattern. It was his own. He noticed that under pressure -- particularly in the first weeks of the role, with the press scrutiny, the share price commentary, and the internal scepticism that he was 'an inside hire who would change nothing' -- he defaulted to proving his technical credentials. He over-explained. He answered questions before they were fully asked. He used technical depth as a shield against the question of whether he could lead.

The cost of the pattern was specific. Senior leaders who needed to bring him problems learned, in the first weeks, that bringing a problem produced a technical response, not a leadership response. They started filtering. The information that reached Nadella narrowed. He has described, in a 2018 Harvard Business Review interview and on the McKinsey podcast 'Inside the Strategy Room' in 2019, the moment he recognised the pattern and the deliberate alternative he installed in its place.

The deliberate alternative was a single behavioural change: when a senior leader brought him a problem, he would ask one question before responding.

"What do you need from me to move this forward?"

The change was small. It was not framed as transformation. He has said publicly that it took him roughly six months to make the new pattern automatic.

Microsoft's market capitalisation when Nadella took over was approximately 314 billion US dollars. By the end of 2019, it had crossed 1 trillion. Nadella has consistently credited the cultural shift -- not the cloud bet, not the Activision acquisition -- as the foundation that made everything else possible.

The dynamic to analyse

This case is not about Microsoft. It is about a leader who had a default pattern under pressure, recognised what it was costing him, and designed a specific behavioural alternative.

The questions below ask you to examine that design discipline -- and to apply it to one of your own patterns.
