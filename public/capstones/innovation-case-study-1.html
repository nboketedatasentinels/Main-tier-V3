The case · read first
GE, 2011-2018 -- Immelt, Predix, and the leadership transition
Collapse
Background

In 2011, Jeff Immelt, then CEO of General Electric (GE), launched the company's Industrial Internet strategy. The vision was to transform GE from an industrial conglomerate into a digital industrial company by combining its physical machine portfolio (jet engines, gas turbines, medical scanners, locomotives) with software analytics that captured and acted on the data those machines produced. Immelt was unambiguous in his framing: GE would be a 'top 10 software company by 2020.'

The centrepiece of the strategy was Predix -- a software platform GE began developing to connect industrial machines, capture their data, and run analytics on that data at scale. GE established a new business unit, GE Digital, in 2015 to house Predix and the broader software effort. The investment was substantial: across roughly six years, GE spent approximately 4 billion US dollars on the Predix platform and the digital transformation more broadly. Public statements through 2015 and 2016 projected digital revenues of 15 billion US dollars by 2020.

In June 2017, GE's board ousted Immelt and named John Flannery as CEO. Flannery inherited GE Digital and the Predix platform alongside multiple other strategic and financial pressures. In November 2017, Flannery announced cuts of approximately 400 million US dollars to the 2018 GE Digital spending plan and revised the 2020 digital revenue projection downward from 15 billion to 12 billion US dollars. He called a two-month timeout for developers to address Predix's technical problems. He committed to focusing the platform on existing industrial sectors rather than continuing the cross-industry expansion Immelt had pursued. GE's stock price fell 56% during 2018.

Flannery's own tenure as CEO lasted approximately 14 months. He was replaced in October 2018 by H. Lawrence Culp Jr., who continued to retrench GE Digital. The Predix-as-platform-for-industry vision did not survive the leadership transition. GE Digital continued to exist but at a fraction of the scope and ambition originally articulated by Immelt. In retrospective interviews and a 2019 MIT Sloan paper co-authored with Vijay Govindarajan, Immelt acknowledged that the original framing had been too ambitious for what the organisation could absorb -- and that the thesis had been articulated in language too closely tied to his personal sponsorship.

The dynamic to analyse

This case is not a story about whether industrial AI is technically viable. Many GE competitors and partners (Siemens, ABB, Schneider Electric) continued to invest in similar industrial software platforms during and after the GE Digital retreat. It is a story about a transformation thesis that was articulated with specificity to one CEO's persona and presence -- and that did not survive the organisational stress test of his departure.

The questions below ask you to identify the thesis articulation choices that made it fragile to leadership change, and to design a thesis articulation that would have transferred.

One specific decision -- not a portfolio of moves. The reasoning has to be 2017-defensible, meaning a board would have approved it on the evidence available in June-November 2017, before the 56% stock decline. 'Communicate better' is not a decision. The test is whether your counterfactual rests on hindsight or on logic Flannery could have argued in front of the GE board on day 30.
