One-Page Proposal

Identify a real opportunity in your current scope — one you've spotted but haven't pitched. Frame it for the audience that controls the decision. Strategic link, business case, specific ask. One page. The artefact a senior leader could read in 90 seconds and either say yes, no, or "tell me more."

Capstone Part A · Closes Think Like an Owner
What good looks like
A One-Page Proposal that holds up to BQA scrutiny.
  • The opportunity is real and from your own scope — specific enough that someone in your organisation could go look at it.
  • The cost of inaction is quantified — in money, hours, strategic risk, or reputational cost.
  • The audience is named — a specific decision-maker, not "leadership."
  • The ask is specific — a number, a timeline, a decision being requested.
  • The artefact is under one page when printed. Length discipline is part of the assessment.
Section 01
Outcome 1 · Opportunity identification

The Opportunity

Name a specific inefficiency, gap, or improvement opportunity in your current scope. Concrete enough that someone in your organisation could go look at it.
Not "we should improve our customer experience." Specific. The report nobody reads. The handover process that costs three days every month. The vendor we're paying for software 80% of users have stopped using.
Section 02
Outcome 1 · Cost of inaction

Cost of Inaction

What does it cost the organisation to leave this unaddressed? Quantify if you can.
Owners spot opportunities AND quantify the cost of leaving them on the table. This is the section that separates a complaint from a proposal.
Section 03
Outcome 2 · Audience-matched framing

Audience

Who specifically controls the decision? What language do they respond to?
A proposal pitched to "the leadership team" lands nowhere. Name them. Name the priority you'll link to. Name the words they use.

Digital Edge · AI Application

Build your 3-slide internal pitch in 15 minutes

Week 2 standardised Digital Edge for this Capstone
Why this matters: When you pitch your own idea, you cannot see it the way your audience does. An AI prompt forces you to describe the idea to someone who knows nothing about it.
The prompt (copy-paste into Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini)
You are a strategic communication coach. I'm pitching an internal opportunity to a senior decision-maker. I'll describe: (1) the opportunity in plain terms, (2) the audience and what they care about, (3) the cost of inaction, (4) the specific ask. Build me a 3-slide pitch skeleton: (Slide 1) the hook — one sentence that earns 90 seconds of their attention. (Slide 2) the case — 3 bullets that make the business argument. (Slide 3) the ask — one specific decision being requested with a date. Then audit my version: where am I assuming knowledge they don't have? Where am I burying the ask? Where is the language mine when it should be theirs? My input: [paste your Section 01, 02, 03, and 04]
How to apply this to your Capstone: Run the prompt with your Sections 01-04 as input. The four reflection questions below ask you to evidence your evaluative judgement — Outcome 6 evidence.

Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Read your proposal artefact (Section 04) aloud. Don't add context. Listen to your peer's reaction.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • If you sent this to your decision-maker tomorrow morning, what's the most likely response — yes, no, or "let me think about it"?
  • The strategic link — is it actually in the strategic plan, or did you make it sound like it is?
  • The ask in your proposal — is it for a decision, or for "support"?
