The Vision Sentence Test

One repeatable vision sentence. 15 to 20 words. Tested with three independent listeners over 24 hours. The point is not the sentence that lands well in the slide deck. The point is the sentence that team members are still able to repeat 24 hours after they heard it. If they cannot, the sentence is not the vision -- it is rhetoric. The data from three real tests is the Practical.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • The sentence is 15 to 20 words. If it is longer, it is not a sentence -- it is a paragraph wearing a sentence's clothes.
  • Three independent listeners ran the 24-hour test. Real people, real time gap, no rehearsal in between.
  • The 24-hour-later recall is captured verbatim where possible -- their words, not your interpretation of what they said.
  • The Practical reports the data faithfully -- including the listener who could not recall it, or recalled it wrong.
  • If a revision is needed, the revised version addresses the specific failure mode the test surfaced -- not a general 'make it shorter' edit.

Step 2
Write the Sentence

One sentence. 15 to 20 words. Designed to be repeatable in your absence -- by someone who heard it once. No clauses inside clauses. No vendor language. No 'leverage' or 'unlock'. The sentence has to do one thing: name what survives if the transformation works, in language a frontline team member could carry into a customer conversation.

Step 3
Run the Three 24-Hour Recall Tests

Three independent listeners. Share the sentence with each of them once -- in conversation, not in a deck, not in writing. Then 24 hours later (within an hour either side) ask them to repeat it back. Their words. Their pauses. The substance is what matters -- not whether they got the wording exactly right. Capture verbatim where you can. Honest about which test passed and which did not.

Step 4
Read the Data and Decide on Revision

Three tests, three pieces of data. The Practical is what they reveal. Two checks. First: across the three, what survived and what did not? The pattern is the data, not any single test. Second: based on the pattern, do you revise the sentence -- and if so, how does the revision address the specific failure mode the tests surfaced? Honest about whether the original sentence is portable or not.

AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Sentence and the Tests

Vision sentences fail in two predictable places -- the practitioner tests with three listeners who already know the transformation (so recall is artificially high), and the revision is a general 'make it shorter' edit instead of one that addresses what the data actually surfaced. AI as an outside reader catches both. Use it before you submit.

1
Test the Sentence Without Context
Ask AI to repeat the sentence back without any context -- and see what an outside reader hears. The biggest failure mode is the sentence that only works if you already know the transformation.
Paste this prompt
I am testing a vision sentence for an AI-era business transformation. Here is the sentence with no context: [paste]. Three checks. (1) Read the sentence out loud once and tell me what you can repeat back five seconds later. (2) Without knowing what industry I am in, what would you guess the transformation is about? (3) Are there any words in the sentence that an external reader would interpret in two opposite ways depending on their frame? Tell me what is portable about the sentence and what depends on the listener already being inside the transformation.
What is portable? What depends on context the listener already has?
2
Test Whether the Revision Addresses the Failure Mode
Ask AI to test whether the revised sentence is a real revision (addresses what the tests surfaced) or a cosmetic one (just shorter or smoother).
Paste this prompt
My original vision sentence: [paste]. The data from three 24-hour recall tests: [paste a brief summary -- what survived, what did not, what was reframed]. My revised sentence: [paste]. Pressure-test the revision on three criteria. (1) Does the revised sentence address the specific failure mode the tests surfaced -- or is it just shorter and smoother in general? (2) If the original sentence had a word that got reframed by listeners (e.g. 'in under two minutes' became 'fast'), did the revision keep the listener's word, or did the practitioner reinstate the original word out of attachment? (3) Is the revised sentence different enough that the next round of recall tests would produce different data, or close enough that you would expect the same drift? Tell me whether this is a real revision or cosmetic.
Real revision or cosmetic? What would make it more real?
AI Lens
Three months from now, after the revised sentence has been spoken at four town halls and reproduced in twenty-six middle-management briefings, what is the version that will be coming back to you in the 24-hour recall test? And is that version still the vision, or is it the operational metric your bonus structure rewards?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Vision Sentence Tests fall apart at the same place every time -- the practitioner tests with three listeners who are too close to the transformation (so recall is artificially high), or the revision is a tidy-up that does not address the failure mode the data surfaced. A peer who has run their own test can hear both at the same speed an assessor will. Bring this to your peer-to-peer call this week.

What to bring

Read out the original sentence first -- once, with no context. Ask your peer what they remember thirty seconds later. If they get most of it back, the sentence is in the right shape; if they get the wrong half, the sentence is mis-weighted. Then read out the three recall summaries and the revised sentence. Ask your peer whether the revision addresses what the tests surfaced -- or whether it is a different sentence that happens to be shorter.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • Your three listeners -- were any of them too close to the transformation to give you a fair test? If yes, the data is partly artefact, not signal.
  • The element that did not survive recall -- the 'two minutes' or whichever specific phrase dropped -- was that the right thing to lose, or are you defending its loss because the alternative is admitting the sentence was too long?
  • The revised sentence -- if you ran three new tests with three new listeners next week, what would you predict the recall data to look like? If you cannot predict, the revision is not yet calibrated.
