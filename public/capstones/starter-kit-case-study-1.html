The case · read first
Eastman Kodak, December 1975 — and the four decades that followed
Collapse
Background

Steven Sasson, an electrical engineer at Eastman Kodak, built the first working digital camera prototype in December 1975. The camera weighed 3.6 kilograms, took 23 seconds to capture a 0.01 megapixel black-and-white image, and stored it on cassette tape. Sasson presented the prototype internally to senior Kodak executives in 1976.

The reception was polite, but the technology was shelved. Sasson has said publicly, including in a 2008 New York Times interview and a 2015 oral history with the Computer History Museum, that the executives' first response was:

"But it's filmless photography. Why would anyone want to look at their pictures on a television set?"

Kodak held the foundational patent on digital photography. The company later licensed the patent to competitors including Canon, Nikon, and Sony, generating an estimated 3 billion US dollars in royalties between 1991 and the patent's expiry in 2007. By the early 2000s, the same competitors had used digital photography to displace Kodak's film business.

Kodak filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on 19 January 2012. By that point, the company's photographic film business had collapsed from its 1996 peak revenue of approximately 16 billion US dollars to under 1 billion. Sasson left Kodak in 2009. He was awarded the United States National Medal of Technology and Innovation in 2009 by President Obama for inventing the digital camera.

The dynamic to analyse

This case is not a story about a missed technology bet. It is a story about a senior practitioner who saw an opportunity in his own organisation's scope, built a working prototype to demonstrate it, presented it to the people who controlled the decision, and failed to win the room.

The questions below ask you to examine why the pitch did not land, and what an audience-matched proposal would have looked like.
