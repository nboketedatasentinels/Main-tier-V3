Pattern Profile

Name how you actually default under pressure -- not the version of yourself you wish showed up. One pattern. Three real moments where it ran. The belief underneath. This is the foundation of everything else in the Journey, so it has to be honest. The pattern you avoid here is the pattern that will keep costing you.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • One specific pattern -- not "I get stressed." A behavioural default that shows up in observable ways.
  • Three real moments from the last 90 days. Specific people, specific decisions, specific words.
  • The cost of the pattern named in concrete terms -- a relationship, a decision, a meeting, a moment of credibility.
  • The belief underneath named honestly. The flattering version is rarely the true version.
1
Step 1
Name the Pattern

When the pressure lands at work -- a stakeholder pushes back, a deadline tightens, a decision goes sideways -- something happens to how you show up. That something is your pattern. Name the one that runs you most often. Specific behaviours, not feelings. "I get anxious" is a feeling. "I over-explain to anyone who pushes back" is a pattern.

01
The Pattern in One Sentence
Behavioural. Specific. Observable. The version a colleague would recognise if you described it to them.
02
When It Shows Up
The trigger conditions. Not where it lands -- what sets it off. Be specific.
2
Step 2
Three Real Moments from the Last 90 Days

A pattern you can only describe abstractly is one you don't yet see clearly. Three specific moments from the last 90 days where this pattern ran. Real people, real meetings, real words. If you can't think of three, the pattern you've named in Step 1 is probably wrong -- come back and refine it.

01
Moment 1
When, who was there, what triggered it, what you did, what it cost.
02
Moment 2
When, who, trigger, what you did, what it cost.
03
Moment 3
When, who, trigger, what you did, what it cost.
3
Step 3
The Cost of This Pattern

If this pattern is just a quirk, you wouldn't be working on it. It's costing you something -- credibility, relationships, decisions, energy. Name what it's actually cost in concrete terms. Not "it makes me feel bad about myself" -- what has it cost you in your actual leadership work.

01
What It's Cost
Concrete. Recent. Specific. The thing you wouldn't want to admit to a peer but is true.
02
What You're Avoiding
The harder cost -- what are you NOT doing because of this pattern? The opportunities you're walking past, the conversations you're not having.
4
Step 4
The Belief Underneath

Patterns don't run on their own. There's a belief driving each one -- often unspoken, often something you'd disagree with intellectually but are still living out. Name the belief. The flattering version of the belief is rarely the true one. "I want to be thorough" is the flattering version. "If they don't fully understand my thinking, they won't trust me" is closer to the truth. Go past the flattering version.

01
The Flattering Belief
What you'd say if someone asked. What you'd put in a 360 review. The respectable version.
02
The True Belief
What's actually running. The belief that explains the behaviour. Often something about safety, worth, or the cost of being wrong.
AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Pattern

When we're naming our own patterns, we tend to soften the edges -- we describe a version we can live with, not the version that's actually running us. AI as an outside voice can hear the gap between what you've written and what your three moments actually show.

1
Test the Pattern Against the Moments
Ask AI to compare the pattern you named against the three moments you described. The pattern should be visible in all three. If it isn't, the pattern is wrong or the moments are wrong.
Paste this prompt
I'm working through a leadership self-awareness exercise. The pattern I've named for myself under pressure is: [paste your Step 1 pattern]. Here are three real moments where I think this pattern showed up: [paste your Step 2 moments 1, 2, 3]. Read these carefully. (1) Is the pattern I've named actually visible in all three moments? (2) Is there a different or more specific pattern that's even more visible? (3) Is there something I'm describing as the pattern that's actually a symptom of a deeper one? Be specific and unflinching.
What did AI surface? Will you tighten the pattern?
2
Pressure-Test the Belief
Ask AI to challenge your stated true belief -- is it actually the truth, or is it still a more polished version of the truth? Sometimes the third draft is the real one.
Paste this prompt
Here is the true belief I've named as driving my pattern under pressure: [paste your Step 4 true belief]. Push on this. (1) Is this the actual driver, or is it still a relatively polished version of something rawer underneath? (2) What would a more honest version of this belief sound like -- the one I might be flinching from naming? (3) What does this belief assume about my worth, my safety, or my place in the room? Don't be gentle.
What did AI push you toward? Will you adjust your true belief?
AI Lens
As you start using AI more in your transformation work, the AI will increasingly be in the room with you in the moments your pattern runs. What's the risk of using AI to outsource the moments that trigger your pattern -- and what would you have to keep doing yourself to stay anchored as a leader?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Pattern work is where senior practitioners are gentlest with themselves. We name the version we can live with, not the version that's actually running us. A peer who's done their own version of this work can hear the difference -- if you're brave enough to read it aloud.

What to bring

Read your Step 1 pattern, your Step 4 true belief, and just one of your three moments aloud. Don't add commentary. Don't soften before you read. Listen to your peer's first reaction -- the one before they say anything kind.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • Of the three moments you described, which one was hardest to write down? That's probably the one closest to the real pattern.
  • Your true belief -- did you write what's true, or what's true enough to admit out loud?
  • What would change about how you show up next week if you genuinely believed this pattern was no longer running you?
