The Connection Blueprint

An audience-matched persuasion artefact written for a specific named executive who controls the decision on your transformation. Four sections -- Position, Now, Stake, Survival -- in language matched to that executive's actual priority at this moment in the business cycle. The Blueprint should be sendable to that executive as is, with no further softening.
Outcomes assessed: LO1 (Analyse the room walked into; apply the Door Practice), LO2 (Design and demonstrate the Connection Blueprint)
Word count: 600-800 words total across all sections.
Capstone Part A of 2 · Combined Capstone = 50% of competence pass
What good looks like
A Blueprint sendable to a named executive -- audience matched, anchored to the right moment in the business cycle, defensible without your personal explanation.
  • The named executive is a real person you could send this to -- by role and ideally name -- not a hypothetical.
  • Position anchors to the audience's actual top concern at this moment, not the practitioner's.
  • Now is anchored to a specific business cycle moment (a board cycle, a regulatory window, a competitor move, an earnings cycle), not generic urgency.
  • Stake is named in language the named executive would actually use about their own work -- not transformation jargon.
  • Survival names what survival of the transformation requires from this audience in the next 90 days, with specific, time-bound, accountable asks.
  • A peer reading the Blueprint could predict what the named executive would say in response.

Section 01
Outcome 2 · Position

Position -- where the transformation programme stands today

State Position with specifics: where the transformation programme stands today, in language matched to the named executive's actual top concern (not yours). Identify the executive at the top of the section -- by role, ideally by name. Anchor to the audience's perspective, not the head office or programme team perspective.
Audience first. State the executive (role, name if known), then their top concern at this moment. Position then describes the programme's actual state in language the executive would use to describe it -- not transformation team language. Specifics matter: numbers, milestones, what is and is not in flight. Avoid press-release framing; the executive can read that elsewhere.

Section 02
Outcome 2 · Now

Now -- the moment in the business cycle that makes this the right time

State Now: the moment in the business cycle that makes this the right time to act. Anchor to a specific business cycle moment for the named executive -- a board cycle, a regulatory window, a competitor move, an earnings cycle, a budget cycle. Avoid generic urgency.
The Now anchor is a real calendar event -- a date that exists on the executive's calendar regardless of your transformation. Generic urgency ('the market is changing fast', 'we need to move now') does not meet the bar. The test: if the executive looked at their calendar, the moment you name should be visible there.
Suggested length: 130-170 words 0 words
Section 03
Outcome 2 · Stake

Stake -- what is at stake for the named executive

State Stake: what is at stake for the named executive specifically -- not for the organisation in general, not for the transformation programme, not for the practitioner. Use language the executive would use about their own work, including risk, accountability, reputation, and outcome.
Stake is read from the executive's perspective. The test: would the executive recognise this as their actual stake, or as the transformation team's view of what their stake should be? Two failure modes: collapsing 'organisational stake' and 'executive stake' (different); writing in transformation jargon rather than the language the executive uses internally.
Suggested length: 150-200 words 0 words
Section 04
Outcome 2 · Survival

Survival -- what survival of the transformation requires from this audience

State Survival: what survival of the transformation requires from the named executive in the next 90 days. Specific, time-bound, accountable. Survival is not vision language -- it names what this executive must do (or not do) for the transformation to survive. The asks are operational and observable.
The asks are time-bound (named dates) and accountable (the executive can succeed or fail at them). Vision language ('lead the change', 'champion the work') does not meet the bar. Two operational tests: an executive could read the asks and put them on their calendar; a peer could check whether the asks were met without ambiguity. The strongest Survival sections also name what survival does not require -- removing scope creep from the executive's plate.
Suggested length: 180-250 words 0 words
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, walk a peer through the Blueprint.

The Connection Blueprint is meant to be sendable to the named executive. The fastest test is whether a peer who knows the executive (or knows how executives like them think) can predict what the response would be.

Questions for your peer to ask you
  • Is the named executive a real person you could send this to, or a hypothetical?
  • Is the Now anchored to a specific calendar event for that executive, or to generic urgency?
  • Does Stake read in the executive's language about their own work, or in transformation team language?
  • Could the executive read the Survival section and put the asks on their calendar without further interpretation?
