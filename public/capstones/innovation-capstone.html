The Transformation Operating Model

A single integrated operating model for innovation and AI deployment decisions at enterprise scale. Six sections that put together the AI Operating Hour discipline, the transformation thesis, the capability map, the data foundation audit, the adoption curve read, and the AI integration discipline. The Operating Model should be defensible to a CEO or board reviewer who was not in the room when the original decisions were made.
Outcomes assessed: LO1 (Apply the AI Operating Hour discipline), LO2 (Construct a transformation thesis that survives a CEO change), LO3 (Analyse and map AI-era capabilities), LO4 (Evaluate the data foundation honestly), LO5 (Evaluate the adoption curve), LO6 (Integrate AI tools with judgement)
Word count: 1500-2000 words total across all sections.

What good looks like
An Operating Model that reads as one running document -- six sections drawing on the Journey practicals, defensible to an incoming CEO or board reviewer without your personal explanation.
  • The AI Operating Hour in Section 1 is a recurring habit with a specific calendar slot, a substantive question held, a real blind spot surfaced, and an observable decision change -- not a one-off reflection.
  • The Transformation Thesis in Section 2 names underlying business reasoning, not technology fashion -- defensible to a hypothetical incoming CEO who was not in the room.
  • The Capability Map in Section 3 distinguishes claimed from observable capability with concrete evidence per row, including at least one structural gap that is not closeable in 90 days.
  • The Data Foundation Audit in Section 4 -- the heaviest section -- names what the use case actually needs, what the organisation has, the specific gap (including one being downplayed), the cost-to-close, and a defensible recommendation.

  • The AI Integration in Section 6 demonstrates evaluative judgement -- what was kept, what was rejected, why -- including instances where you overrode the AI suggestion with your own judgement.
  • The six sections read as one connected operating model, not six exercises stapled together. An incoming CEO could read it and act on it without your personal explanation.

Section 01
Outcome 1 · AI Operating Hour

AI Operating Hour discipline

State your AI Operating Hour discipline as a recurring practice. Include the recurring calendar slot (specific day, specific hour), the substantive question you have been holding, what the discipline has surfaced that execution had been hiding, the decisions you are now making differently as a result, and the cadence you are committing to going forward. The Operating Hour is ongoing, not a one-off.
Carry forwardBuild on the AI Operating Hour Log (Practical 1). Update against your actual practice over the Journey, not the snapshot from Week 1.
Recurring means a calendar slot, not aspirational time. The substantive question is one you have actually been holding, not a hypothetical. What the discipline surfaced is a real blind spot -- something you did not see clearly before. The decision change is observable: a colleague could notice the difference. Anything that depends on you remembering to do it is not a discipline yet.

Section 02
Outcome 2 · Transformation Thesis

Transformation Thesis -- defensible to an incoming CEO

State the transformation thesis for your current programme in language that does not depend on a specific sponsor's persona. Include the stated outcome the transformation is in service of (the underlying business reason, not the technology fashion), the three to five claims the thesis depends on with one sentence each on why each is defensible, the named risks if any one claim turns out to be false, and the explicit test that would tell an incoming leader whether the thesis is still on track.
Carry forwardBuild on the Transformation Thesis Practical (Practical 2). Strengthen against the stress-test of an incoming CEO who was not in the room when the original decisions were made.
The test of sponsor-independence: an incoming CEO who has never met the current sponsor reads the thesis and understands the underlying business reasoning. Technology fashion ('we are using AI') does not survive the stress-test; underlying business reasoning ('we are reducing reconciliation cost in retail credit decisioning') does. The dependent claims are named explicitly, not buried in narrative; each claim can be stress-tested individually.

Section 03
Outcome 3 · Capability Map

Capability Map -- claimed versus observable

Map your organisation's capabilities against the requirements of one specific stated AI use case. Three columns: capability claimed (in strategy documents, board papers, or vendor pitches); capability observable (with one piece of evidence per row -- a working pilot, a named team, a specific deployed system); gap (what would need to be true to close it). Surface at least three capabilities where the gap is significant, including at least one structural gap that is not closeable in 90 days.
Carry forwardBuild on the Capability Map Practical (Practical 3). The use case must be specific enough to drive capability requirements (not "AI in operations" but a specific deliverable with a specific scope and a specific date).
Observable evidence cells need to point to concrete artefacts -- a system, a team, a deployment -- not aspirational language. The structural gap is the test of honesty: an organisation that has only 90-day-closeable gaps is either small or is not telling the truth. The map should be uncomfortable in places; if it is not, you have written a strategy document, not a map.
Suggested length: 280-380 words 0 words
Section 04
Outcome 4 · Data Foundation Audit

Data Foundation Audit -- the heaviest discipline of the Journey

Audit the data foundation against the requirements of the same use case from Section 3. Cover: the data the use case requires (sources, freshness, quality, lineage, governance permissions, latency, completeness); the data the organisation actually has against each requirement, with evidence; the foundation gap; the cost of closing the gap (time, cost, governance work, regulatory dependencies); the recommended call (deploy now, deploy after foundation work, or do not deploy and reframe the use case). Include at least one gap the organisation is currently downplaying or framing as 'not yet a problem'.
Carry forwardBuild on the Data Foundation Audit Practical (Practical 4). This is the heaviest discipline in the Journey -- the section where most use cases fail and where most practitioner judgement gets tested.
The discipline is in distinguishing the data the use case actually needs from the data the organisation wishes it had. Evidence cells need to point to named systems, named data stewards, real lineage diagrams -- not 'we have customer data'. The downplayed gap is the test: every organisation has one, and the practitioner's job is to surface it before the use case fails publicly. The recommended call is defensible to a CFO who is going to ask about cost.
Suggested length: 320-420 words 0 words
Section 05
Outcome 5 · Adoption Curve Read

Adoption Curve Read -- cited metric versus underlying signal

Read the adoption curve of one specific AI deployment in your organisation. Include: the publicly cited adoption metric; the underlying signal that metric is masking (pilot-stage performance, narrow use case, opt-in user base, novelty effect, supervisor workaround, selective measurement); the genuine usage signal you believe is closer to the truth; the specific signals that would shift your read in either direction; the recommended action.
Carry forwardBuild on the Adoption Curve Reading Practical (Practical 5). The reading must be specific enough that an incoming CEO could verify or challenge it.
The cited metric is named with the figure if available. The masking pattern is named from the framework -- one of the five candidates, not all of them. The shift-signals are concrete: a particular user group, a particular case type, a particular metric crossing a particular threshold. 'More data would help' is not a shift-signal. Substituting pessimism for analysis fails the discipline; the read needs to be defensible from evidence.
Suggested length: 240-320 words 0 words
Section 06
Outcome 6 · AI Integration

AI Integration -- judgement, not adoption

Select one Digital Edge exercise output from the Journey (the thesis stress-test prompt, the capability gap prompt, the data foundation audit prompt, the adoption signal prompt, or another). Name what you kept from the AI output, what you rejected, and why -- including any instance where you overrode the AI suggestion with your own judgement.
The discipline is evaluative -- not 'AI gave me good ideas'. Name a specific output, what you kept and why, what you rejected and why. If everything was kept, you have summarised, not evaluated. The override case is the strongest evidence: a moment where the AI suggested one move and you chose another, with the reasoning named.
