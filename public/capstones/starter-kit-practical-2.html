Stakeholder Position Paper

Take the opportunity you chose in Practical 1 and analyse the decision-maker who controls action on it. Half a page. Three things you need to know about them before you write the proposal: what they actually care about, what's blocking them, and what you'll need to address to win their support.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • Decision-maker named by specific role (not just "my manager"). Real person, real seat.
  • Their priorities sourced from real signals -- their last townhall, recent emails, public commitments. Not imagined.
  • Honest read on what blocks them. AI-readiness concerns, political risk, capacity -- not just "budget."
  • Clear link between the analysis and the proposal you'll write. You're preparing the ground, not just describing it.

AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test Your Read

Stakeholder analysis is where practitioners most often deceive themselves -- we project our own logic onto the decision-maker and miss what they actually care about. AI can challenge that. Here's how.

1
Stress-Test the Decision-Maker's Priorities
Ask AI to challenge your read on what the decision-maker actually cares about. The goal is to find priorities you've assumed but not evidenced.
Paste this prompt
I'm analysing a decision-maker who controls approval for an AI/digital transformation opportunity. Their role is [paste from Step 2]. The three priorities I think they hold right now are: [paste from Step 3]. For each priority, challenge me: what evidence am I relying on, where might I be projecting my own logic, and what alternative priority might actually be more important to them given their role and current organisational context?
What did AI challenge? What will you reconsider?
2
Anticipate the Counter-Arguments
Ask AI to play your decision-maker and push back on your strongest argument. Find the holes before the meeting does.
Paste this prompt
I'm preparing to make a proposal to a [role from Step 2]. My strongest argument is: [paste from Step 5]. Play this decision-maker. They've been burned before by AI/digital transformation projects that overpromised. They're cautious. Push back hard on my argument -- give me three specific counter-arguments they would make, in their voice, and tell me which one is the hardest to answer.
What's the hardest counter-argument? How will you handle it in the proposal?

Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Stakeholder analysis is where you're most at risk of fooling yourself. A peer who doesn't know your decision-maker can hear the gaps in your logic faster than you can. Bring this Practical to your peer-to-peer call this week.

What to bring

Read your Step 2 (Decision-Maker), Step 3 (Priorities), and Step 4 (Blockers) aloud. Pause. Ask your peer to ask the three questions below. Don't defend your answers -- listen for where you sound less certain than you wrote.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • For each priority you named -- what's the actual evidence? Could you point to a specific email, meeting, or comment that backs it up?
  • Are you sure you've identified the real decision-maker? Or is there someone behind them who'll actually decide?
  • Of the three blockers, which one are you secretly hoping won't come up in the meeting? That's the one to address most carefully in the proposal.
