Trigger Map

Take one trigger from your Carrying Inventory and map it in detail. The body signal that fires first. The old action that follows automatically. The pause point -- the moment between the signal and the action where intervention is possible. The deliberate alternative. Specific enough that you could hand it to yourself under pressure and act on it.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • Body signal named with specificity -- where, what sensation. Not "I feel stressed."
  • Pause point identified -- the moment between signal and action where intervention is possible.
  • Deliberate alternative connects back to your Protocol Card from Week 2.
  • The Map is usable in 30 seconds under pressure -- not theoretical.

Step 1
Choose the Trigger You're Mapping

From your Carrying Inventory in Practical 4, you named what you've been carrying into high-stakes rooms. One trigger fires that pattern most reliably -- the moment, the kind of comment, the kind of room where the old behaviour activates fastest. That's the one you're mapping.

01
The Trigger
In one sentence -- the specific situation that fires the old pattern.
02
Why This One
Of all the triggers you could map, why this one? What makes it the most worth the work?

Step 2
Map the Four Stages

A trigger doesn't fire instantly. There's a sequence -- body signal first, then the old action, with a tiny window in between where intervention is possible. That window is the Pause Point. Most leaders don't see it because they've never named it. You're naming it now.

The Trigger Sequence
01
Body Signal
Where in the body. What sensation. Treat the body as data.
02
Old Action
What happens automatically -- the behaviour you don't choose.
PAUSE POINT
03
Pause Point
The window between signal and action. Name what makes it possible.
04
Deliberate Alternative
From your Week 2 Protocol Card -- the protocol you'll run instead.
3
Step 3
Make the Map Usable Under Pressure

A Map you can read in calm conditions is not the same as a Map you can read 1.5 seconds before you speak. This step is the difference. You're testing whether the Map you wrote in Step 2 is actually deployable -- or whether it's a journal entry pretending to be a tool.

01
The 30-Second Read
Write the shortest version of your Map -- the one you'd actually re-read 60 seconds before walking into the meeting. Three lines maximum.
02
First Real Test
When is the next high-stakes moment where this trigger is likely to fire? Not "sometime soon" -- a specific upcoming meeting.
AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Sharpen the Map

Trigger maps are most often vague where vagueness costs the most -- in the Pause Point and the Deliberate Alternative. AI as an outside reader can hear the gap between what you've written and what would actually work in the room.

1
Test the Pause Point
Ask AI to evaluate whether your Pause Point is real -- or whether you've described a wish, not a window.
Paste this prompt
I'm building a Trigger Map for use under pressure. The trigger is: [paste your Step 1]. The body signal is: [paste your body signal]. The old action is: [paste your old action]. The Pause Point I've identified is: [paste your pause point]. Pressure-test the Pause Point. Specifically: (1) Is this a real moment of agency, or have I described an idealised version of myself noticing the signal? (2) What would have to be true for me to actually use this Pause Point in real time -- not in reflection? (3) What's the most likely reason I'd miss the Pause Point in the moment, and what would help me catch it instead?
What did AI flag? How will you tighten the Pause Point?
2
Stress-Test the Alternative Under Real Conditions
Ask AI to roleplay the senior leader who fires this trigger and respond to your Deliberate Alternative as they would. Find out if the alternative actually works in the room, not just on the page.
Paste this prompt
I'm preparing for a high-stakes meeting where a specific trigger is likely to fire. The trigger: [paste Step 1]. My Deliberate Alternative is: [paste your alternative from Step 2]. Play the senior leader who fires this trigger -- I've described them as: [one or two lines about who they are and how they typically push]. Run a 60-second exchange. They challenge me. I use the alternative. They respond. Don't soften it. Push the way they would. Tell me where my alternative breaks under their pushback.
Where did the alternative break? What will you tighten?

Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Trigger maps fall apart at the same place every time -- the Pause Point. Most practitioners describe a Pause Point that requires them to be a different person to use. A peer who's done their own Map can hear when the Pause Point is real and when it's wishful. Bring this Practical to your peer-to-peer call this week.

What to bring

Read the four stages aloud, in order. Do it in the same tone you'd use to brief yourself before walking into the trigger meeting. If you sound calmer reading it than you feel in the actual room, the Map needs work. Listen to your peer's reaction at the Pause Point -- that's where the gap usually is.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • Is the body signal specific enough that you'd actually catch it -- or is it the kind of signal you'd only notice in retrospect?
  • The Pause Point -- did you write the version that requires you to be calm, or the version that works when you're already activated?
  • If the trigger fires tomorrow morning unannounced, would you actually run this Map? Or is it a Map for the version of you who's prepared, and not the version who's surprised?
