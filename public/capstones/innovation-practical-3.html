The Capability Map

Three columns. Claimed capability — as it appears in strategy documents, board papers, vendor pitches. Observable capability — what the organisation can actually demonstrate today, with concrete evidence per row. Gap — the specific delta and what would need to be true to close it. The Practical surfaces at least three significant gaps for one specific stated AI use case. Distinguishing the claimed from the observable is the discipline. The map is the artefact.

What good looks like
Aim for these as you go.
  • The use case is specific — not 'AI in operations' but 'AI-driven fraud detection scaled to the South African retail bank by Q3' — because abstract use cases produce abstract capability requirements.
  • The map covers at least five capabilities relevant to this specific use case — not a generic capability framework. Capabilities are picked because the use case depends on them.
  • Observable capability cells are filled with concrete artefacts: a working pilot, a named team, a specific deployed system. If the cell reads 'we have built capability in X', it is aspirational not observable.
  • At least three capabilities are flagged as significant or structural gaps — including at least one structural gap (not closeable in 90 days) and at least one gap the organisation is currently downplaying.
  • An incoming CEO reading the map could state which gaps make the use case undeliverable as currently scoped, without you in the room to defend it.

Step 1
Frame the Use Case Specifically

Before the map, the use case. Specific enough to drive capability requirements — the test is whether two different practitioners reading the use case would derive similar capability lists. If the use case is abstract ('AI for customer experience'), the map will be abstract. The capability gap discipline only works when the use case has scope, scale, timeline, and accountability attached.

Map Five Capabilities

Five capabilities the use case depends on. For each one: claimed capability (with source), observable capability (with concrete evidence), and the gap (with what would need to be true to close it). Rate each gap minor / significant / structural.

The Capability Map
Five capabilities · three columns · gap rated against closeability and timeline
Minor: closeable in 30 days
Significant: closeable in 90 days, costed
Structural: not closeable in the use case timeline

The map is data. The Practical is what you do with it. Two checks. First: which gap has the organisation been downplaying? Second: based on the map, what is the defensible call to leadership — deliver as scoped, deliver after foundation work, or reframe?

AI-Powered Activity

Use AI to Pressure-Test the Map

Capability Maps fail in two predictable places — the observable column reads as paraphrased claim, and the practitioner picks a comfortable downplayed gap rather than the politically uncomfortable one. AI as an outside reader catches both.

1
Test Whether the Observable Column Is Actually Observable
Paste this prompt
I am submitting a five-row capability map for an AI use case. The 'observable' column for each row is: [paste]. Pressure-test on three criteria. (1) For each row, is the observable cell describing something with a name, a date, or a number that an external party could verify? Or is it describing the work in language that sounds concrete but cannot be verified? (2) Does any observable cell repeat the language of its paired claim cell, with one word changed? (3) Of the five rows, which observable cell would an incoming CEO push back hardest on? Tell me which observable cells are still claims and rewrite the weakest one with verifiable evidence.
2
Test Whether the Downplayed Gap Is Honestly the Hardest One
Paste this prompt
I have mapped five capabilities, three rated significant or structural. I named [Capability X] as the gap the organisation is downplaying. The political reason: [paste]. Pressure-test: (1) Of the three significant/structural gaps, is the one I named the politically hardest to surface — or the one most aligned with my own positioning? (2) For each of the other two, what is the political reason it might also have been downplayed? (3) If a peer asked 'who specifically would be uncomfortable if you surfaced this gap publicly', could I answer with named individuals? Tell me whether the named downplayed gap is the honest one.
AI Lens
Six months from now, when the foundation work has progressed and the rollout has scoped to chat-only by FY26 Q4, which capability that you rated 'minor' today will have aged into a 'significant' gap because the use case definition itself shifted? And what does that prediction say about the temporal stability of capability mapping as a discipline?
Take This to Peer-to-Peer

Before you submit, talk it through.

Capability Maps fall apart at the same place every time — the observable column ends up paraphrasing the claimed column with one word changed, and the gap rated 'structural' is the comfortable one to call structural rather than the politically expensive one.

What to bring

Read the use case definition first. Ask your peer if they could derive a similar capability list from your use case alone. Then read the observable column for each row. Ask your peer to point at any cell that reads as a claim with the verb tense changed. Then read the downplayed gap.

Three questions for your peer to ask you
  • Of your five observable cells, are any of them paraphrased versions of their paired claim cell with one word changed?
  • Your downplayed gap — can you name the specific person or function who would be uncomfortable if it was made public this quarter?
  • Your defensible call — if leadership accepted it tomorrow, what would have to change in the FY26 narrative?
