Q1: Yes. As an R&D engineer his scope was precisely to explore what charge-coupled devices could do, and building a working filmless capture prototype sat squarely inside that mandate. The miss was not the discovery but its placement: a 0.01MP, 23-second, cassette-tape device was a feasibility proof, not a market-ready product. Identifying it was correct for his role. Owning the commercial decision was never his scope, which is exactly why framing it for those who did own that decision mattered far more than the demo itself.

Q2: Kodak earned roughly $3B in digital patent royalties between 1991 and 2007, so the technology itself paid. The cost of inaction sits in the core business: film revenue fell from a ~$16B peak in 1996 to under $1B, a decline of more than $15B in annual revenue. That collapse, driven by the very digital shift Kodak had patented, ended in Chapter 11 on 19 January 2012. Net of the $3B in royalties, inaction cost the company its primary revenue engine and its independence.

Q3: Decision-maker: Kodak's senior film and marketing leadership, chaired in effect by the Chairman and CEO (Walter Fallon era), whose priority in 1976 was protecting the dominant, high-margin film and chemical franchise. Pitching "filmless photography" attacked that priority head-on, so it read as a threat. The reframe is to anchor it inside the franchise they were paid to defend. Sasson could have said: "This is a fifteen-to-twenty-year horizon technology that will eventually let someone capture images without film, so the question is whether Kodak owns that transition and monetises it, or a competitor does it to us."

Q4: Opportunity: fleet utilisation is stuck at 67% and my weekly utilisation report is ignored. Decision-maker: the COO. The risk is that I am the analyst who flagged idle capacity for months while the report became wallpaper. I would reframe it as recoverable cost and capacity: "At 67% we are paying for the equivalent of roughly 79 vehicles we are not using, so closing half the gap to 80% frees enough capacity to absorb peak demand without buying trucks." I would lead with the dormant capital, attach one decision the COO can make this month, and stop sending a passive weekly number.
