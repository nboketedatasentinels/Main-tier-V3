q1: First, formalise the Modernisation programme governance: convert its status, milestones and in-flight spend into a written charter signed off by the executive committee and tabled with the Audit Committee, because a programme that exists only in delivery momentum can be cancelled by a single newsflash, whereas a chartered programme requires a documented, accountable decision to stop. Second, lock asset and contract custody: place the e-Central assets, in-flight project budgets and vendor contracts under a register with named accounting officers and a change-control gate, so that any halt or reallocation triggers a recorded approval trail under Treasury and PFMA rules rather than an undocumented stand-down.

q2: A known risk is one people can see; a managed risk is one that has an owner, a control and an escalation path before it lands. Here the risk was widely known: Hore, Rabie, Scheepers and Burger all understood what dismantling Modernisation would cost. But it was not managed. There was no consultation, no governance gate the decision had to pass, and no contemporaneous escalation to the Board, Auditor-General or Minister. Burger's "the curtain had fallen" captures the gap: knowledge sat with practitioners while authority sat elsewhere. The proof of the failure to manage is that the objections surfaced in 2018 to the Nugent Commission, four years late, after >R66m in projects, >R22m in unaccounted e-Central assets, a >R200m irregular Gartner tender and a further R164m Bain contract, ending in a ~R1bn repair bill. Known and unmanaged is indistinguishable from unknown until the loss is irreversible.

q3: To the Chair of the SARS Audit Committee: I am formally escalating a material risk that the Modernisation programme, which by 2014 had made SARS one of Africa's most respected tax authorities and is on track to complete in 2015, may be halted without governance review, placing more than R66m of in-flight projects and over R22m of e-Central assets at immediate risk and exposing SARS to irregular-expenditure and continuity exposure. I am asking the Committee to require, before any decision to pause, descope or cancel the programme, a documented business-case review and a formal stop-gate approval with named accounting-officer sign-off, and to confirm that request in writing to the Commissioner by month-end.

q4: Healthy disagreement is on the record, addressed to the decision-maker, and tied to evidence and a constructive alternative; political behaviour is off the record, addressed to everyone except the decision-maker, and aimed at being proven right later. As Operations Analytics Lead, suppose a new COO wants to scrap the utilisation reporting that finally exposed our 67% problem and revert to gut-feel dispatching. Healthy disagreement is a written note to the COO showing the recoverable-capacity figure, requesting a 60-day parallel run before retirement, and accepting the decision if it still goes ahead. Political behaviour would be staying quiet in the meeting, briefing depot managers in the corridor that the COO is making a mistake, and saving the evidence to vindicate myself when utilisation slips. The test is simple: did I put the objection where the authority sits, in writing, at the time? SARS shows the cost of doing the opposite.
